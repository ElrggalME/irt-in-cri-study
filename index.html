About the Project

What is the IRT-in-CRI Study?

Catheter-related infections (CRI) are among the most common and costly healthcare-associated infections worldwide, significantly increasing patient morbidity, mortality, length of hospital stay, and treatment costs.

The IRT-in-CRI Study applies AI-powered infrared thermography — a non-invasive, radiation-free imaging modality — to detect thermal signatures of early infection at catheter insertion sites, before clinical signs appear.

Funded by the Saudi NIH under the Epidemics Research and Biotechnology Initiative, the project is hosted at Umm Al-Qura University and involves partner institutions in the UK and Germany. Project activities at the involved universities commence in November 2026.

"Our goal is to build a validated, deployable AI model that any hospital equipped with an infrared camera can use at the bedside — transforming infection surveillance from reactive to proactive."

— Dr. Mahmoud Essam Elrggal, Principal Investigator

Background & Rationale

Catheter-related infections contribute to significant patient morbidity and healthcare costs. Current diagnostic approaches rely on clinical signs and laboratory markers that become positive only after infection is established. Infrared thermography detects localised heat from inflammation — preceding visible signs by 24–72 hours. Despite its theoretical promise, IRT has not been rigorously validated for CRI detection.

Funded under the Saudi NIH Epidemics Research and Biotechnology Initiative — linking healthcare-associated infections to Saudi Arabia's pandemic preparedness and patient safety agenda.

Study Objectives

  • Assess sensitivity and specificity of AI-powered IRT for early CRI detection vs. standard clinical assessment and microbiology cultures.
  • Develop, train, and validate a deep learning model for automated IRT image classification.
  • Monitor thermal patterns in CRI patients receiving antimicrobial therapy — establishing IRT as a treatment monitoring tool.
  • Build a labelled IRT image dataset from Saudi clinical settings.
  • Produce high-impact Q1 publications and evidence-based recommendations for IRT adoption in Saudi infection control protocols.

The IRT-in-CRI study is a prospective, observational, multi-centre clinical study conducted at Umm Al-Qura University, Makkah. Adult patients (≥18 years) with indwelling vascular catheters will be recruited and followed for the duration of catheterisation.

Concurrent clinical assessments, microbiological cultures, and laboratory markers will serve as the reference standard. Infrared images are captured at enrolment and at pre-specified intervals. The AI model will be trained and validated using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and transformer-based architectures.

Thermal Imaging: A calibrated mid-wave infrared camera captures images of catheter insertion sites under standardised conditions (ambient temperature, patient positioning, distance) at enrolment and pre-specified follow-up intervals.

AI Model Development: CNNs (ResNet, EfficientNet) and vision transformers trained on the labelled thermal image dataset. Transfer learning employed to address dataset size constraints. Performance evaluated using AUC-ROC, sensitivity, specificity, PPV, and NPV.

Data Management: All clinical and imaging data captured in REDCap, stored on secure institutional servers. AI analysis in Python (TensorFlow, PyTorch); statistical analysis in R/Stata.

Expected Significance

  • First validated AI-powered IRT model for CRI detection in a Middle Eastern clinical setting.
  • A publicly available labelled thermal image dataset for CRI research.
  • Evidence base for updating Saudi clinical practice guidelines on CRI surveillance.
  • Minimum 3 peer-reviewed publications in Q1-indexed journals.
  • A scalable, cost-effective, radiation-free monitoring tool applicable to resource-limited settings.
